[48.19, 63.17] Foreword

[48.19, 103.56] The National Emergency Medicine Programme presents the Irish Children’s Triage System (ICTS) for the prioritisation and

[48.19, 115.56] assessment of paediatric patients presenting to Emergency Departments (EDs) in Ireland. The system supports safer, more

[48.19, 127.56] effective, timely ED care and reflects the importance of child and family experiences of emergency care. The ICTS makes a very

[48.19, 139.56] significant contribution to the suite of clinical tools developed by the National Emergency Medicine Programme to drive

[48.19, 151.56] improvement in the safety, quality and value of emergency care in Ireland.  

[48.19, 175.56] The triage of children in EDs is an established standard of care in international and national practice. However the triage of

[48.19, 187.56] children can be difficult compared to adults and additional triage parameters are recommended to reflect age-related physiological

[48.19, 199.56] differences, children’s presenting signs and symptoms, significant paediatric co-morbidities and common Paediatric Emergency

[48.19, 211.56] Medicine diagnoses. 

[48.19, 235.56] The development of the ICTS was prompted by the triage experiences of front-line ED nurses and doctors caring for children and

[48.19, 247.56] advanced by the Emergency Nursing Interest Group (ENIG) of the National Emergency Medicine Programme as a safety and

[48.19, 259.56] quality improvement initiative.  The development and testing of the ICTS has been conducted under the guidance of a Steering

[48.19, 271.56] Group composed of stakeholders from within Emergency Medicine and senior clinicians across a number of hospitals. The ICTS

[48.19, 283.56] has undergone extensive stakeholder consultation and the National Emergency Medicine Programme and Office for Nursing and

[48.19, 295.56] Midwifery Services Director are therefore pleased to endorse the recommendations outlined in this document and recommend

[48.19, 307.56] that ICTS be adopted as the national standard of triage for children and should be adopted in all EDs in Ireland that see paediatric

[48.19, 319.56] patients. Implementation of the ICTS will contribute significantly to paediatric patient care in our EDs and will promote safety,

[48.19, 331.56] quality of care, improved access and patient experience in emergency care in Ireland.

[48.19, 63.17] 1. Purpose 

[48.19, 103.56] This document describes the Irish Children’s Triage System (ICTS), a child-specific triage tool that should be followed for the

[48.19, 115.56] prioritisation and assessment of paediatric patients presenting to an Emergency Department (ED) in Ireland. 

[48.19, 139.56] Locally developed paediatric triage systems had been in place in two Paediatric EDs in Dublin, Our Lady’s Children’s Hospital,

[48.19, 151.56] Crumlin (OLCHC) and Temple Street Children’s University Hospital, (TSCUH). Formal evaluations of these systems indicated that

[48.19, 163.56] they were safe and effective tools. As a quality improvement initiative, the National Emergency Medicine Programme (EMP)

[48.19, 175.56] through its Emergency Nursing Interest Group (ENIG) convened an ICTS project subgroup comprising senior nurses from seven

[48.19, 187.56] EDs (children’s only and mixed) and specialists in Paediatric Emergency Medicine. This group collaboratively produced the

[48.19, 199.56] national ICTS, based largely on the pre-existing tool in use in OLCHC with input from TSCUH.

[48.19, 223.56] The work of the project subgroup was overseen by a National Steering Group of key stakeholders and experts in the area of

[48.19, 235.56] paediatric emergency care. The ICTS was successfully piloted and evaluated in six EDs across the country in 2013, namely:

[48.19, 63.17] 2. introduction

[48.19, 103.56] The term triage comes from the French verb ‘trier’ meaning to separate, sort, sift or select. It was a concept originally developed

[48.19, 115.56] by Dr Dominique Jean Larrey, a military surgeon during the Napoleonic Wars, and was used to sort soldiers wounded in battle

[48.19, 127.56] for the purpose of establishing treatment priorities. 

[48.19, 151.56] Changes in the health care delivery system in the United States forced EDs to consider alternative ways of handling an increase

[48.19, 163.56] in the number of patient attendances during the 1950s and early 1960s (Gilboy et al 2005). EDs recognised that a method to sort

[48.19, 175.56] patients and identify those needing immediate care was required. This provided the impetus to have ED triage systems in place.

[48.19, 187.56] Doctors and nurses who had used the triage process effectively in the military environment introduced triage into civilian EDs.

[48.19, 199.56] The transition of the triage process from the military environment to US EDs was considered very successful (Gilboy et al 2005).

[48.19, 223.56] Across the world EDs are faced with increasing numbers of patients presenting faster than they can be seen. Longer waits for

[48.19, 235.56] care make the use of reliable, valid triage systems essential to patient safety (Storm-Versloot et al 2011). Triage is the rapid and

[48.19, 247.56] preliminary assessment which identifies and sorts patients based on their need for or likely benefit from immediate medical

[48.19, 259.56] treatment (Fitzgerald et al 2009). Additionally, there are patients who will not require major resources for assessment and treatment

[48.19, 271.56] and could be seen in a low-intensity (fast-track/ambulatory unit) area by a doctor or an Advanced Nurse Practitioner. Identifying

[48.19, 283.56] these patients as they present permits the ED to maximise resources and clinical space and allow appropriate resources to be

[48.19, 295.56] invested in sicker patients at the same time as the less acute and less resource-dependent patients have their needs met.

[48.19, 319.56] International healthcare systems in the United Kingdom (Manchester Triage System 1997, 2006 & 2014), Canada (Canadian

[48.19, 331.56] Paediatric Triage and Acuity Scale 2001 & 2008), and Australia and New Zealand (Australasian Triage Scale, ACEM 2013) have

[48.19, 343.56] designed national triage systems to attend to patient needs based on acuity of presentation, improve patient safety and enhance

[48.19, 355.56] satisfaction. Although the United States has a variety of triage tools (3-tier to 5-tier systems), the most commonly used triage

[48.19, 367.56] system in the US is the 5-tier Emergency Severity Index (1998, 2012). Five-level triage systems have been shown to be more

[48.19, 379.56] reliable and valid than three-level systems (Travers et al 2002, College of Emergency Nursing, Australia 2012).

[48.19, 63.17] 3. the generic triage Process

[48.19, 103.56] To ensure transparency and uniformity, a formally structured triage system for ED patients is advocated. Internationally, the most

[48.19, 115.56] commonly used triage processes are 5-tiered in nature and classify the acuity of a patient’s condition based chiefly on the patient’s

[48.19, 127.56] presenting complaint and findings on assessment (Murray et al 2004, ACEM 2013). Acuity rating levels are used to prioritise

[48.19, 139.56] patients for care and characteristically involve assigning a numeric score to patients, such as triage category 1 (most acute) to

[48.19, 151.56] triage category 5 (least acute). The presenting complaint determines which flowchart is followed. Each flowchart is based on a

[48.19, 163.56] five-step decision process that uses general and condition-specific discriminators at each step to assign patients to one of the

[48.19, 175.56] five triage categories (Storm-Versloot et al 2009). The triage decision and acuity rating allocated to the patient determines the

[48.19, 187.56] ideal maximum time to first contact with the treating clinician (Mackway Jones et al 2006) and this prioritisation influences and

[48.19, 199.56] ultimately affects patient safety. It is imperative therefore, that patients in need of urgent care are identified immediately, as

[48.19, 211.56] adverse outcomes may otherwise occur while the patient is waiting. Mackway-Jones et al (2006, 2014) maintain that triage is a

[48.19, 223.56] fundamental element of ED clinical risk management. Inherent in the belief that triage is of value is the assumption that the

[48.19, 235.56] outcome of some patients will be improved if they are seen sooner whilst other patients will not be adversely affected by delays

[48.19, 247.56] in their care. 

[48.19, 271.56] Triage is frequently the first instance of clinical contact for patients and their carers presenting to the ED. It is consistently the

[48.19, 283.56] point at which emergency care begins in the acute hospital setting. Clinical decisions made by triage nurses require complex

[48.19, 295.56] cognitive processes. The ability to make efficient clinical judgments and decisions is critical, and the quality and accuracy of

[48.19, 307.56] triage decisions are central to appropriate and timely clinical care. Some authors suggest that response times (interval from time

[48.19, 319.56] of triage categorisation to treating clinician review) are ideals and not standards, based on the need for timely interventions that

[48.19, 331.56] improve outcomes (Warren et al 2001, PricewaterhouseCoopers and the Health Administration Corporation 2011). However

[48.19, 343.56] ACEM (2013) suggests that the triage category outlines the maximum time that a patient should wait for a treating clinician

[48.19, 355.56] assessment. 

[48.19, 379.56] Triage also allows for the allocation of a patient to the most appropriate assessment and treatment area and contributes information

[48.19, 391.56] that helps to describe case-mix in the ED (ACEM 2013). Patients who are identified as safe to wait longer may be seen by another

[48.19, 403.56] caregiver such as an Advanced Nurse Practitioner (Veen & Moll 2009). The triage process should form the start of a documented

[48.19, 415.56] care pathway for each individual patient (Comhairle na nOspideal 2002).

[48.19, 71.63] Internationally, it is recommended that the triage assessment should take no longer than two to five minutes to obtain sufficient

[48.19, 83.63] information to determine urgency and identify any immediate care needs (Warren et al 2001, Gilboy et al 2005, Gerdtz et al 2007,

[48.19, 95.63] ACEM 2013). However Travers (1999) demonstrated that this target was only achieved 22% of the time. The triage process tends

[48.19, 107.63] to incur delays especially in the younger age group of attendees. 

[48.19, 131.63] Recording of vital signs in children, for example, is time consuming due to difficulty with cooperation (age-related), cognitive

[48.19, 143.63] ability and comprehension. Privacy also needs consideration as exposing children for assessment and examination is mandatory

[48.19, 155.63] in a number of circumstances, particularly where rashes need to be assessed or small infants need to be weighed and this can

[48.19, 167.63] only be performed in a private cubicle. Parental anxiety is another factor that cannot be overlooked and is often reduced by the

[48.19, 179.63] triage nurse. The commonly internationally accepted times associated with triage are based on adult triage.

[48.19, 203.63] The assessment of initial features, presenting problem, general appearance, physiological findings, age of the child and significant

[48.19, 215.63] past medical history should determine whether a patient is a category 1 (immediate) or 2 (very urgent) and accordingly should

[48.19, 227.63] be seen within the appropriate timeframe. A more thorough assessment may be required to determine lower triage categories.

[48.19, 239.63] The National Emergency Medicine Programme Report (2012) recommends that 95% of patients are triaged within 15 minutes

[48.19, 251.63] of presentation to an ED.

[48.19, 63.17] 4. Background to irish children’s triage system

[48.19, 103.56] 4.1 Nursing assessment 

[48.19, 127.56] Prior to 2011, there were three triage systems in use for triaging children in Ireland: Manchester Triage System (MTS) in the

[48.19, 139.56] majority of EDs and two modified triage systems designed, tested and audited locally in OLCHC and TSCUH by local Triage

[48.19, 151.56] Committees consisting of Consultants in Emergency Medicine and senior ED nurses. 

[48.19, 175.56] In June 2011, the Clinical Nurse Managers 3 (CNM3) from OLCHC and TSCUH were invited to present their triage tools to the

[48.19, 187.56] Emergency Nursing Interest Group (ENIG). At ENIG meetings senior emergency nurses had expressed the view that MTS had

[48.19, 199.56] limitations as a tool when triaging children in their departments as many of the decisions made regarding the allocation of triage

[48.19, 211.56] categories were dependent on nurses’ experience with no consideration given, in particular, to the importance of abnormal pulse

[48.19, 223.56] and respirations in children. As a quality improvement initiative, a proposal was made by ENIG to the Emergency Medicine

[48.19, 235.56] Programme Working Group that a subgroup should be set up to design an Irish Children’s Triage System (ICTS). The objective

[48.19, 247.56] was to standardise a formal, comprehensive triage tool that would improve consistency between users and be suitable for triaging

[48.19, 259.56] children in all EDs with paediatric attendances in Ireland. The ICTS subgroup comprised of senior nurses from the three paediatric

[48.19, 271.56] hospitals and four hospitals with both adult and paediatric attendances. Professor Ronan O’Sullivan and Dr Carol Blackburn (both

[48.19, 283.56] OLCHC) were the medical leads and all Consultants in Emergency Medicine across the three paediatric EDs in Dublin supported

[48.19, 295.56] the project. 

[48.19, 319.56] A comprehensive analysis of the international contemporary triage systems (MTS, Canadian Paediatric Triage and Acuity Scale)

[48.19, 331.56] had been previously undertaken by the two hospitals who had designed local triage tools. A review of the literature identified

[48.19, 343.56] three options available when systems of triage are introduced into EDs (Fortes Lahdet 2009): 

[48.19, 368.38] 
 [70.87, 368.38] Adopt an existing, well-recognised and validated method; 

[48.19, 381.19] 
 [70.87, 381.19] Develop a national triage model that is suited to national needs; 

[48.19, 394.01] 
 [70.87, 394.01] Design a local model that reflects specific needs and interests. 

[48.19, 430.01] 4.2 manchester triage system – Emergency triage (mts)

[48.19, 454.01] The MTS tool (1997, 2006 and 2014) is an example of a 5-tiered triage system which was referenced considerably whilst

[48.19, 466.01] developing the two local child-specific triage tools. It uses a series of flow charts for various "presentations" with key

[48.19, 478.01] "discriminators" to determine the triage category. MTS however, is primarily an adult triage tool (Beveridge et al in 1999,

[48.19, 490.01] PricewaterhouseCoopers, Health Administration Corporation 2011) and authors suggest that general scales are less helpful (or

[48.19, 502.01] reliable) at the extremes of age. In 2011, MTS (2006) had 50 flowcharts on the most common presenting complaints to ED;

[48.19, 514.01] however only 7 of these were specific flow charts for triaging children (abdominal pain in children; crying baby; limping child;

[48.19, 526.01] irritable child; shortness of breath in children; unwell child; worried parents). The current version of MTS (2014) has 55 flowcharts

[48.19, 538.01] with 3 additional flowcharts which relate to children (unwell baby, unwell newborn, abused or neglected child). 

[48.19, 562.01] In MTS, there are no child age-specific parameters available for abnormal pulse or respirations for children. The recommendation

[48.19, 574.01] that ‘age appropriate definitions of bradycardia and tachycardia should be used in children’ (MTS 2014, p 161) was considered

[48.19, 586.01] by the ICTS project sub-group to be too subjective for the assessment of children. In addition, ‘increased work of breathing’

[48.19, 598.01] (MTS 2014, p149) explains increased respiratory rate without providing guidance on age-related clinically relevant parameters. 

[48.19, 634.01] 4.3 canadian Paediatric triage and acuity scale guideline (Paedctas)

[48.19, 658.01] PaedCTAS (Warren et al 2001, 2008) was the only child specific triage system identified and is also a 5-tiered triage scale.

[48.16, 670.01] PaedCTAS was considered to be less user-friendly than MTS but included an age-specific physiologic parameter assessment.

[48.16, 682.01] Warren et al (2008) identified that abnormal respiratory rate and heart rate may be the only indication of underlying sepsis or

[48.16, 694.01] impending shock. With this in mind the 'Vital Signs Reference Grids’ previously adapted in OLCHC from the age-specific

[48.15, 706.01] physiologic parameter assessment used in PaedCTAS were incorporated into the Irish Children’s Triage System (Appendix 1.1).

[48.16, 730.01] Education and training in PaedCTAS system of triage was not readily available in Ireland and this was considered an essential

[48.16, 742.01] element in developing a formalised triage tool.

[48.19, 63.17] 5. the development of the irish children’s triage system (icts)

[48.19, 103.56] Neither MTS nor PaedCTAS on their own were considered to fully address the needs of the Irish paediatric patient population or

[48.19, 115.56] the users of the system, thus the merits of both systems were identified and merged into one child-specific triage tool, namely

[48.19, 127.56] the Irish Children’s Triage System (ICTS).

[48.19, 151.56] It was agreed to use a similar format to that of MTS as the flow charts used in MTS were thought to be user friendly and easy to

[48.19, 163.56] follow. It was also considered appropriate to build on a system that was already familiar to staff rather than introduce a completely

[48.19, 175.56] new system that would require substantial re-education. Furthermore, ICTS adapted the use of numbering of categories and ideal

[48.19, 187.56] time targets from MTS rather than target times recommended in the Canadian or Australasian triage tools. Finally, the physiologic

[48.19, 199.56] parameters assessment of PaedCTAS, as modified previously for use in OLCHC, was incorporated into the new tool (5.2 Tables

[48.19, 211.56] 1,2,3).

[48.19, 235.56] Hereafter, ICTS was developed using flowcharts representing the 22 most common paediatric presenting complaints to Irish

[48.19, 247.56] EDs. As the presenting signs and symptoms of childhood illnesses can be insidious in nature and the presenting complaint may

[48.19, 259.56] therefore not always fit neatly into one of the defined flowcharts, two additional default flowcharts of Unwell Child and Unwell

[48.19, 271.56] Infant (including pyrexia) were developed that would be comprehensive enough to capture all potential clinical risk. It is vital,

[48.19, 283.56] however, that if there is an alternative flowchart more specific to the patient’s presenting complaint that this specialised flowchart

[48.19, 295.56] is used and the flowcharts Unwell Child and Unwell Infant (including pyrexia) are only used where a more appropriate flow chart

[48.19, 307.56] is not available. 

[48.19, 343.56] 5.1 special case guidelines

[48.19, 367.56] There are some conditions where patients may present and appear well but their underlying condition indicates that these patients

[48.19, 379.56] can deteriorate at a very rapid rate. With this in mind patients with a history of metabolic disorder, Sickle Cell Disease, patients

[48.19, 391.56] with a tracheostomy and breathing difficulties or patients with ventriculo-peritoneal shunt problems should never receive a lower

[48.19, 403.56] acuity than category 2.

[48.19, 427.56] In addition, the triage of patients with a co-existing medical problem or extremes of age provides particularly difficult challenges

[48.19, 439.56] (Veen et al 2008). Patients with significant co-existing illness and co-morbidity are a group in which assessment and management

[48.19, 451.56] is frequently complex, even with normal childhood illnesses. The ICTS project subgroup gave these children specific consideration

[48.19, 463.56] (as well as children and babies < 3 months of age) and decided that in the absence of indicators for a higher category, these

[48.19, 475.56] patients should never be categorised lower than category 3.

[48.19, 63.17] 8. Educational requirements for Performing icts

[48.19, 103.56] There is no consensus in relation to the minimum clinical experience a nurse needs prior to undertaking triage. Some systems

[48.19, 115.56] recommend that the nurse must be an ‘experienced’ nurse without quantifying what defines a nurse as ‘experienced’ (Beveridge

[48.19, 127.56] et al 1998, O’Neill & Molczan 2003, ACEM 2005, Gilboy et al 2005). Morgan & Whelan (2000) recommended that the nurse

[48.19, 139.56] should have at least 18 months of emergency nursing experience. However, Wulp et al (2008) have not found any association

[48.19, 151.56] between work experience and interrater reliability at triage. Gilboy et al (2005) recommend a 2-4 hour mandatory educational

[48.19, 163.56] programme for staff whilst Maldona & Avner (2004) recommend 6 hours of formalised triage training. 

[48.19, 187.56] Crellin & Johnson (2003) previously suggested that nurses not specifically trained in paediatrics may find triaging paediatric

[48.19, 199.56] patients challenging due to lack of expertise in the physiological and cognitive differences in children. This potentially leads to

[48.19, 211.56] children being allocated a lower priority triage category (category 5) less often.

[48.19, 235.56] While it is acknowledged that some ED experience is essential to assist the nurse in identifying a critically ill patient, the ICTS

[48.19, 247.56] Project Subgroup consider that education of staff in the use of the tool is essential prior to undertaking unsupervised triage.

[48.19, 259.56] Education of staff involves formal teaching sessions, supervised triage episodes and completion of a competency sign off booklet.

[48.19, 271.56] However, if there are concerns in relation to a nurse’s performance in relation to appropriate/accurate triage decisions, he/she

[48.19, 283.56] may undertake the triage workbook again to ensure further support/evaluation is provided. It is recommended that ongoing

[48.19, 295.56] concerns regarding triage performance should be managed through local nursing practice development structures.

[48.19, 63.17] 9. Pilot Phase

[48.19, 103.56] ICTS was piloted in six sites from March 2013 – February 2014. In preparation for the pilot phase of ICTS, permission was sought

[48.19, 115.56] from the Directors of Nursing of each participating hospital. No data protection or patient confidentiality risks were identified.

[48.19, 139.56] All pilot sites identified local champions who would lead on training in their own ED. Formal teaching sessions, which comprised

[48.19, 151.56] an eight-hour study day were organised for each participating pilot site. This education was provided by the Clinical Nurse

[48.19, 163.56] Managers (CNM3) from the three paediatric EDs who had been pivotal in developing the ICTS tool. 

[48.19, 187.56] The champions train-the-trainer approach ensured that training of all staff was standardised across the six sites. Clinical training

[48.19, 199.56] and instruction of nurses has been previously identified as assisting in optimising the usage and interpretation of triage systems

[48.19, 211.56] (Veen & Moll 2009). The pilot phase was managed by the ICTS project subgroup who addressed operational issues and provided

[48.19, 223.56] support to each site. Prior to and during the pilot phase the project subgroup carried out audits, staff questionnaires and inter-

[48.19, 235.56] rater reliability studies, to support on-going evaluation and validation of the tool. 

[48.19, 259.56] The pilot phase was planned to take place over a 3-month period, however, the tool was found to be so beneficial that all six EDs

[48.19, 271.56] opted to continue to use the ICTS after the end of the pilot period. As a result, audit data was gathered from the participating

[48.19, 283.56] hospitals for the period March 2013 - February 2014. The additional audit period allowed the project subgroup to ensure that the

[48.19, 295.56] results of audit were not influenced by seasonal variation. Data was gathered from the six EDs for the year preceding the

[48.19, 307.56] introduction of ICTS (i.e. March 2012 - February 2013). This allowed the allocation of triage categories to be compared pre and

[48.19, 319.56] post introduction of ICTS.

[48.19, 367.56] observations on data results

[48.19, 391.56] Nurses who do not have paediatric training may find the triaging of paediatric patients challenging (Crellin & Johnson 2003). In

[48.19, 403.56] addition, nurses who do not work exclusively with paediatrics may be hesitant to use lower acuity categories (Figures 1a and

[48.19, 415.56] 1b). However, following the ICTS training in the pilot sites there was a notable change in the use of triage category 4 in Hospital

[48.19, 427.56] 1. Hospital 2 shows a small increase in the use of the lower triage categories with little change in Hospital 3. 

[48.19, 451.56] In relation to the three paediatric hospitals (Hospitals 4-6), there was a significant difference in the allocation of triage categories

[48.19, 463.56] between Hospitals 4 and 6 and Hospital 5 but following the introduction of ICTS, the three paediatric EDs showed very similar

[48.19, 475.56] patterns of attendance by triage category. 

[48.19, 499.56] Although a triage tool is not designed to determine whether a child would subsequently require admission to hospital, the ICTS

[48.19, 511.56] project subgroup attempted to establish a correlation between the higher acuity triage categories and admission rates (Figures

[48.19, 523.56] 2a & 2b). Hospital 1 had a reduction in the overall % of lower triage categories 4. Hospitals 2, 4 and 5 had a significant increase

[48.19, 535.56] in the percentage of higher triage category 2 being admitted (admission rate as an overall percentage of admissions). However

[48.19, 547.56] there was little change noted in Hospitals 3 and 6. 

[48.19, 571.56] The admission rate per triage category as an overall proportion of admissions was much more consistent.  Yet again, after the

[48.19, 583.56] introduction of ICTS the three paediatric EDs were very similar in admissions per triage category as an overall percentage of

[48.19, 595.56] admissions.

[48.19, 619.56] The three paediatric EDs were in a position to collect data on children admitted to PICU. Gravel et al (2009) reports that 90% of

[48.19, 631.56] patients admitted to intensive care were categorised as category 1 or 2 (using the Canadian Paediatric Triage and Acuity Scale).

[48.20, 643.56] Prior to the introduction of the ICTS, the 3 paediatric EDs reported a similar categorisation pattern for children admitted to ICU.

[48.19, 655.56] However, at that time, 8% of those admissions to PICU had received a triage category 3 and 2% had received a triage category

[48.18, 667.56] 4 (Figure 3a). Following the introduction of the ICTS, 94% of admissions to PICU were either a triage category 1 or 2 and no

[48.18, 679.56] patient received a triage category 4 or 5 (Figure 3b). The findings in relation to initial acuity assessment and the need for PICU

[48.17, 691.56] beds would suggest that the ICTS tool can identify acutely ill children and ensure that these children receive immediate and

[48.14, 703.56] urgent medical attention.

[48.19, 63.17] 10. limitations of irish children’s triage system

[48.19, 103.56] Triage systems are designed to appreciate the value of human life and health with fairness and the efficient use of resources

[48.19, 115.56] (Fitzgerald et al 2009). Triage should only be used if there is a delay in patients receiving medical attention, thereby reducing

[48.19, 127.56] clinical risk.  Consequently, if there are no patients waiting for medical review, there should be no delay in the patient receiving

[48.19, 139.56] medical attention and the patient should receive a nursing and medical assessment simultaneously. 

[48.19, 163.56] The triage process tends to incur delays, especially in the younger age group of attendees. Therefore, it is recommended that

[48.19, 175.56] consideration should be given to develop a local contingency plan when there are delays for triage to ensure that all patients are

[48.19, 187.56] seen by a nurse or doctor in a timely fashion.

[48.19, 211.56] Triage is sometimes used by hospital managers and service planners as a marker of acuity and complexity of care within EDs,

[48.19, 223.56] hospitals and networks of care. This has led to considerations that patients in ‘lower’ triage categories (triage categories 3-5)

[48.19, 235.56] could be easily and safely managed in alternative healthcare settings. This misuse of triage data is potentially dangerous because

[48.19, 247.56] triage is essentially a tool to facilitate ‘clinical justice’ identifying which patients need to be seen sooner; it is not a marker of how

[48.19, 259.56] complex or qualified that medical care is. 

[48.19, 283.56] One of the major limitations of any triage tool is difficulty in its validation. A number of audits of ICTS have been undertaken to

[48.19, 295.56] ensure that it is a robust and comprehensive tool (outlined in the previous section). Further audit and evaluation is planned as

[48.19, 307.56] EDs throughout Ireland commence implementation of ICTS. The recommendation from the National Emergency Medicine

[48.19, 319.56] Programme and ICTS project subgroup is that continuous audit should be carried out at local level and all variations analysed

[48.19, 331.56] locally and discussed within ED multidisciplinary teams. Learning from local audits should be shared with the National Emergency

[48.19, 343.56] Medicine Programme and used to inform further refinement of the tool.

[48.19, 63.17] 11. conclusion

[48.19, 103.56] The National Emergency Medicine Programme consistently strives to improve the care that is delivered to patients, their families

[48.19, 115.56] and carers who attend EDs throughout the country. The triage system outlined in this document aims to assist in ensuring that

[48.19, 127.56] children who attend Irish EDs receive the best possible effective and timely care. 

[48.19, 151.56] Overall feedback on the pilot phase of the project was very positive from staff in all participating EDs. Emergency nurses found

[48.19, 163.56] that ICTS was a safe and user friendly tool which guided them to quickly recognise sick children presenting to their department. 

[48.19, 187.56] In conclusion the recommendation of the National Emergency Medicine Programme is that the Irish Children's Triage System

[48.19, 199.56] should be adopted as the national standard of triage for children and should be implemented in all EDs in Ireland with paediatric

[48.19, 211.56] attendances.
